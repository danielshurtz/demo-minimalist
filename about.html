Our Story

Sōen means
"garden."

A place cultivated slowly, with patience. Where nothing is forced and everything has its season.

001

The Beginning

Chef Yuki Tanaka spent seven years cooking in Kyoto — first as an apprentice at a traditional kaiseki restaurant, then as sous-chef at a two-Michelin-starred venue in Gion. She came to Manila in 2015 for what she thought would be a short consulting project. She stayed.

"There's something here," she says of Manila's food scene. "A hunger, an openness. People want to be surprised." Sōen opened two years later, in a converted shophouse in Makati, with 32 seats and no printed menu on the first night.

That first night sold out in 40 minutes and hasn't stopped since.

"Less. Then less again. Until you can't remove anything else — that's when you know it's ready."
— Yuki Tanaka
002

The Philosophy

Sōen follows the Japanese concept of ma — the meaningful use of negative space. On the plate, this means fewer ingredients, more intention. A dish might have three components that together say more than twelve could.

The menu changes daily, anchored by what arrives from our network of farmers and fishers. We work with five families in the Mountain Province for vegetables. Our fish comes from a small cooperative in Batangas. We've been with most of them since day one.

What We Believe

I

Sourcing is half the cooking

We spend as much time with farmers as with recipes. If the ingredient is right, the dish almost makes itself.

II

Restraint over abundance

We don't add. We consider what to remove. Every item on a plate earns its place through contribution, not decoration.

III

The guest sets the pace

A meal at Sōen is yours. We match the energy of the table — never rushing, never disappearing.
